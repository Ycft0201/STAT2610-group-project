Students’ struggle with digital addiction: the 
truth of brain rot
Mustafa Gül1*
Abstract
Background Due to the high level of digital addiction, the term brain rot was chosen as the word of the year for 2024 
determined by a public vote conducted by Oxford University, and it has been used by experts to express the effect 
of consuming an excessive amount of low-quality online content, especially on social media. This study investigates 
the prevalence of brain rot among higher education students in a faculty of education, explores its underlying causes, 
examines its implications for education, and identifies potential preventive measures.
Methods A qualitative case study design was employed to gain an in-depth understanding of the phenomenon. 
The study group comprised 113 students enrolled in various departments of the Faculty of Education at a public 
university in Türkiye during the 2024 to 2025 academic year. Participants were selected using criterion sampling, a 
purposive method designed to identify information-rich cases. Data were collected through observations, semi
structured interviews, and document analysis, and analyzed using content analysis.
Results The findings indicate that nearly all participants experienced symptoms of brain rot, which were attributed 
to emotional factors, habitual behaviors, and environmental, social, and cognitive influences. It has been determined 
that brain rot primarily causes students to experience attention deficits, learning difficulties, mental fog, diminished 
cognitive performance, reduced academic achievement, weakened social relationships, reluctance to attend school 
or engage in educational activities, and decreased time efficiency. Participants recommended several strategies to 
mitigate brain rot, including educational interventions, environmental modifications, digital usage regulation, digital 
detox, and personal self-regulation practices.
Conclusions The results highlight how students’ experiences of brain rot are reflected in their educational lives and 
underscore the measures that need to be taken to prevent its occurrence.
Keywords Digital addiction, Internet addiction, Social media addiction, Brain rot

Introduction
T
he widespread integration of computers, the internet, 
smartphones, and digital platforms into daily life has 
transformed how individuals engage in communication, 
learning, and leisure. These tools are extensively used 
across all age groups for diverse purposes [1]. However, 
their pervasive use, particularly among students, has also 
given rise to various risks, most notably digital addiction 
[2]. Defined as a dependence on multiple forms of digi
tal media, including the internet, digital games, smart
phones, and social networking platforms [3], digital 
addiction is widely recognized as a modern behavioral 
disorder [4].
Users are drawn to digital media for numerous reasons, 
including identity construction, entertainment, escap
ism, information seeking, self-expression, interpersonal 
interaction, stress relief, and staying current with trends 
[5–10]. Moreover, platform architecture, such as infinite 
scrolling, content flow algorithms, and gamified features, 
has been shown to reinforce compulsive use [5, 11].
Research increasingly links digital addiction to a range 
of psychosocial and academic problems, including stress, 
social anxiety, depression, loneliness [12, 13], insomnia, 
eating disorders [14], reduced academic motivation [15] 
and declining academic performance [16]. Studies also 
highlight its role in attention deficits, impaired social 
relationships, and physical symptoms such as back pain 
and fatigue [2, 17, 18].
While digital addiction has been extensively studied 
[19–22], a newly emerging and less explored phenom
enon, brain rot demands urgent academic attention [23]. 
Voted the Word of the Year in 2024 by Oxford Univer
sity Press, brain rot is broadly defined as the cognitive, 
emotional, and behavioral deterioration resulting from 
overexposure to low-quality online content, particularly 
via social media [24]. Though indirectly foreshadowed by 
Henry David Thoreau’s [25], reflections on mental stag
nation in Walden, the term has recently gained traction 
to describe the cognitive fatigue and superficiality char
acterizing digital life in the 21 st century [23].
Brain rot is associated with broader constructs such 
as digital, internet, and social media addiction [26–28]. 
Recent scholarship points to its adverse impact not only 
on individuals’ cognitive and emotional development but 
also on educational and societal systems at large [29, 30]. 
As global concern about its effects grows, scholars have 
emphasized the need for structural educational reform 
to counteract its influence [23]. Brain rot should not be 
understood as a type of addiction. Rather, it refers to a 
condition that arises as a consequence of digital, internet, 
and social media addictions. Conceptually, while addic
tion denotes a behavioral dependence characterized by 
compulsive engagement and withdrawal symptoms, brain 
rot represents the cognitive and affective deterioration 
that follows such addictive engagement. Grounded in 
neuroscientific evidence, brain rot manifests as mental 
exhaustion and a measurable decline in cognitive func
tions and attentional capacity resulting from prolonged 
exposure to excessive digital content [31–33]. At the 
same time, brain rot is an important problem that also 
affects the educational process [16]. Therefore, solutions 
need to be found on a global scale [23]. Teachers are 
faced with the task of combating the threat of brain rot.
In addition, pre-service teachers play a pivotal role in 
shaping society’s digital awareness and literacy. As future 
educators, their attitudes and behaviours towards digi
tal technologies will significantly influence how upcom
ing generations engage with digital media. Therefore, 
examining the digital habits and awareness levels of this 
group is crucial, not only for understanding their per
sonal development but also for ensuring a more digi
tally responsible and conscious educational culture. As 
future educators, their awareness of brain rot, both in 
theory and in practice, is critical to addressing its edu
cational implications. For this reason, this study focuses 
on pre-service teachers to explore how brain rot affects 
their learning experiences and academic functioning. The 
research also seeks to identify contributing factors and 
possible interventions.
Specifically, the study aims to (1) examine pre-service 
teachers’ awareness of brain rot and digital addiction, (2) 
explore the perceived effects of brain rot on educational 
engagement, readiness, and productivity, and (3) iden
tify their suggestions for preventing and mitigating the 
phenomenon. By addressing a timely and underexplored 
topic, this study contributes to the emerging literature on 
brain rot and offers insights for educational stakeholders 
seeking to promote healthier digital habits.
Method
T
his section outlines the research model, study group, 
data collection tools, data analysis, and validity-reliability 
measures.
Research model
T
he present study was conducted using a case study 
design. Case studies are research designs that aim to col
lect in-depth information about real-life, contemporary 
situations and to present the examined case through 
identified themes [34]. This design is frequently preferred 
in practice-oriented fields such as education [35]. In this 
context, the case examined in the current research is 
brain rot. Since the study aimed to reveal how students 
experience brain rot, the reasons behind it, and how 
this situation reflects on their educational processes, 
a case study design was deemed suitable. Case studies 
aim to obtain detailed information about the investi
gated situation by utilizing various data sources such as 
Page 3 of 15 Gül BMC Psychology           (2026) 14:98 
observations, interviews, and documents [35]. In this 
research, these three techniques were used to capture the 
depth of the situation. To ensure the data were not lim
ited or biased and to achieve data triangulation, multiple 
data collection techniques were employed [34].
Patton [36] emphasizes that, to conduct an in-depth 
analysis of a case, it is essential to gather detailed per
spectives from different individuals related to the situ
ation. Accordingly, in this study, the observations and 
opinions of university students from different depart
ments who experience brain rot were examined in detail.
Study group
The study group comprised 113 pre-service teachers 
enrolled in the Faculty of Education at Kahramanmaraş 
Sütçü İmam University, a public university in Türkiye, 
during the 2024 to 2025 academic year. Criterion sam
pling, a purposive method targeting information-rich 
cases aligned with research objectives [36], was used to 
select students who (1) were enrolled in teacher educa
tion programs and (2) reported experiencing symptoms 
of brain rot [36]. Ethical standards were strictly followed. 
Participants’ identities were anonymized using codes 
(K1–K15 for interviews; D1–D113 for documents). As 
shown in Tables 1 and 72 participants were female and 
41 were male, spanning six departments: early childhood, 
elementary, science, English language, social studies, and 
Turkish language education.
Students reported an average of 6 h and 30 min of daily 
unproductive screen use, with only 32 min and 30 s of 
productive use. Face-to-face interviews were conducted 
with 30 students selected from a total of 113 partici
pants. In qualitative research, the sample size is deter
mined by the point at which data saturation is reached 
[37]. Accordingly, the number of interviews in this study 
was established based on the principle of data saturation. 
Data saturation was considered to have been achieved 
when no new codes or themes emerged during successive 
interviews and participant responses began to repeat. 
At this point, the sample was limited to 30 participants. 
Moreover, it was observed that this number of interviews 
ensured sufficient depth, variation, and richness of data 
to address the research objectives comprehensively.
Table 2 shows that the interviews were conducted with 
30 university students studying in 6 different depart
ments of the Faculty of Education. These participants 
are students in the departments of preschool, classroom, 
science, English, social studies, and Turkish teaching. 
20 of the participants are female, and 10 are male. Their 
ages are concentrated in the 19, 20, and 21 age groups. 
According to Table 2, almost all participants mostly use 
screens inefficiently, with inefficient screen time aver
aging 4 h and 38 min per day. The participants’ average 
daily use of screens efficiently is 1 h and 36 min. It is also 
observed that K3, K10, K19, and K25 use screens more 
efficiently, while K29 uses screens equally efficiently and 
inefficiently. During the interviews, students were asked 
again about their efficient and inefficient screen use. 
Many reported spending considerably more time on 
screens for non-educational or unproductive purposes 
than indicated in the documented screen-time records. 
It was determined that the screen time reported in the 
participant observation form differs from the screen time 
presented in the interviews. This striking difference may 
be related to the students being able to confront them
selves more openly while writing and not specifying 
names in the document.
Data collection instruments
Three tools were used to gather research data in the 
study: observation forms, participant documents, and 
semi-structured interviews.
Observation
Researcher and participant observation forms were 
developed by the researcher. The former tracked digital 
usage over three months by instructors; the latter docu
mented six days of individual screen use. Of 140 distrib
uted forms, 113 valid responses were analyzed.

Documents
Documents are written materials providing information 
about phenomena, behaviors, or situations [37]. Partici
pants were asked to write reflective narratives on their 
brain rot experiences. To ensure relevance, the document 
prompts were aligned with research sub-questions and 
reviewed for bias and completeness [37, 38]. The narra
tives explored how students conceptualized brain rot, 
described their experiences, identified its causes and 
educational impacts, and proposed potential solutions.
Interviews
Semi-structured interview protocols were developed 
through literature review and expert input. A pilot with 
five students refined the questions. Of the 113 partici
pants, 30 consented to interviews. All instruments were 
carefully archived after data collection.
Data collection procedure
As outlined in Fig. 1, the study began with a litera
ture review, followed by selection of 140 students. 
Observation forms were administered in six classes, and 
expert feedback was used to refine instruments. Inter
views were conducted with 30 students, and 113 docu
ments were analyzed. Based on these inputs, a participant 
observation form was created and validated by experts.
Data analysis

interpreted, comparisons are made, and a framework for

this study, the content analysis technique and the induc

lyzed in three phases. First, the data obtained through 
the participant observation form were tabulated. The 
table included each student’s daily screen time and the 
efficiency status of this time. The researcher observa
tion form data were used to diversify and substantiate the 
data obtained through the document form and the inter
view form within the framework of the research find
ings. In the second phase, documents from 113 students 
were analyzed using content analysis. In the third phase,

Validity and reliability
In qualitative research, ensuring validity and reliability 
is predicated on conducting the study ethically [35]. To 
carry out this research ethically, the recommendations 
of Christensen, Johnson, & Turner were followed, giv
ing careful attention to participants’ voluntary involve
ment and providing students with detailed information 
about the study [41]. This research was approved by 
the Kahramanmaraş Sütçü İmam University Social and 
Human Sciences Ethics Committee (Decision No. 2025
21, dated 21.03.2025). Participants included in the study 
were provided with detailed information about the pur
pose, process, confidentiality principles, and voluntari
ness of the study. Written informed consent was obtained 
from all participants, and they were informed of their 
right to withdraw from the study at any time. Partici
pants’ identities were kept confidential, with codes such 
as Ö1, Ö2 for participants and D1, D2 for documents 
[42]. Additionally, efforts were made to faithfully reflect 
the research process. To ensure the validity and reliability 
of the study, the four strategies recommended by Lincoln 
& Guba for qualitative research credibility, transferabil
ity, dependability, and confirmability were employed [43]. 
T
he work conducted within this framework is summa
rized below.
Credibility refers to the accuracy of findings and was 
ensured through triangulation, prolonged engagement, 
participant validation, expert review, and researcher 
reflexivity [34, 36]. Data triangulation in this study was 
achieved using observations, interviews, and docu
ment analysis, enhancing the depth and consistency of 
f
indings.
T
he researcher engaged with participants from Sep
tember 2024 to March 2025, observing them in natu
ral settings and collecting in-depth data. Two experts 
reviewed the instruments, and participant validation was 
obtained by confirming the accuracy of data with par
ticipants. The researcher’s role was shared transparently 
with participants to minimize bias and increase trust.
Generalizability is not a goal in qualitative research; 
instead, transferability refers to the extent to which find
ings can be applied to other contexts [35]. To support 
Page 6 of 15 Gül BMC Psychology           (2026) 14:98 
transferability, the research process from sampling to 
data collection and analysis was described in detail, fol
lowing the recommendations of Lincoln and Guba [43]. 
Findings were presented with direct participant quota
tions, and language was kept clear and accessible. Addi
tionally, tables and figures were used to help readers 
evaluate the applicability of results in their own contexts.
Dependability concerns the consistency of findings. To 
ensure this, the researcher and an expert independently 
coded the data and developed themes. Discrepancies 
were resolved through discussion, strengthening the reli
ability of results [34, 40].
Confirmability addresses the objectivity of the research 
and was achieved by grounding all interpretations in par
ticipant data. An audit trail of the entire research process 
was maintained, ensuring transparency and enabling 
external verification [34]. Direct quotations were used 
to reduce researcher bias and ensure that conclusions 
reflected participants’ authentic views. All procedures 
were also documented systematically to allow future 
researchers to track the methodological steps taken.
Findings
This section presents the results derived from observa
tions, documents, and interviews, organized around the 
study’s objectives: participants’ experiences with brain 
rot, its causes, educational impacts, and suggested pre
vention strategies.
Participants’ experiences of brain rot
Findings from all data sources indicated that all 113 par
ticipants experienced symptoms of brain rot. Table 3 
presents one participant’s six-day digital usage, showing 
37 h of unproductive screen time compared to only 1 h 
and 50 min of productive use. Reflecting on this, the par
ticipant stated:
I didn’t know I was this addicted. After observ
ing myself for six days and filling out this form, I 
realized that I need to take precautions. No won
der I have been unable to focus on my studies and 
research. (K4)
In a classroom observation on December 22, 2024, when 
assigned a research task during a philosophy of educa
tion course, the student instead accessed a shopping site 
on their phone. On December 15, she was observed using 
their phone throughout the class break.
Causes of participants’ brain rot
This section presents the findings regarding the underly
ing causes of participants’ experience of brain rot, based 
on thematic analysis. As shown in Table 4, these causes 
are grouped under the themes of emotional causes, 
habitual patterns, environmental causes, social causes, 
and cognitive causes. Among these, emotional factors 
were reported as the most prominent contributors.
Participants frequently identified boredom and the 
desire for relaxation as key emotional drivers. Many 
noted that they turned to digital media to escape monot
ony or to find temporary relief. This is illustrated in K14’s 
reflection:
I get bored easily… I use my phone to relax, but I 
guess that’s what’s rotting my brain.
Similarly, avoiding tasks or seeking inaction was com
monly reported as a coping strategy to escape academic 
or personal responsibilities (K11, D35). Classroom obser
vations supported these accounts; during a session on

Another emotionally driven cause was the feeling of 
lack or disconnection when not using social media. Par

When I don’t pick up my phone, I feel like I won’t be

Some participants also identified emotional motiva
tions behind brain rot, including a desire to escape real
life problems and a need to feel happy in their following 
statements:
When I go through negative experiences in life, I use 
my phone to forget about them. (K5)
Some participants, on the other hand, stated that 
they act based on the feeling of happiness. This situ
ation is reflected in K29’s statements:
When I’m online, I feel happy, like I disappear.

In a subsequent observation conducted on December 10, 
2024, students were again seen spending their breaks on 
their phones, often smiling as they did so, suggesting a 
momentary sense of enjoyment or relief during use.
In terms of habitual causes, many participants men
tioned that they engaged with digital content simply to 
pass the time or as a form of entertainment and rest. K20 
stated: “I have too much free time, I just want to kill it,” 
while K23 described their use as driven by “a need for 
entertainment.” Others framed it as a perceived sense of 
rest (D7). The desire for convenience was also evident, as 
expressed by a participant:
I prefer not to think, I choose convenience, I don’t 
question, I take the easy way out, I’ve lost my read
ing habit. (D23)
Among environmental causes, the COVID-19 pan
demic was frequently cited as a significant contributor 
to increased experiences of brain rot. As one participant 
explained:
T
he pandemic period was very impactful. We were 
locked at home during the pandemic, we had noth
ing to do, so we clung to our phones. (K11)
Participants also highlighted other environmental influ
ences such as peer pressure (D76), the perceived slowness 
of real life (D18), and the appeal and easy accessibility of 
digital content (D18). Finally, the digitalization of edu
cation was noted as a contributing factor. A 20-year-old 
female participant described how academic use of digital 
tools often led to distraction:
Our teachers give us assignments, so I have to check 
my phone and research on the internet, but then I 
end up drifting off to other things. (D12)
T
his pattern was also evident during an observation on 
December 17, when students were encouraged to use AI 
tools for a class activity. While conducting their research, 
many were observed switching between academic plat
forms and social media, reflecting a blurred boundary 
between purposeful engagement and distraction.
In relation to social causes, many participants high
lighted a desire for solitude as a contributing element 
to their experience of brain rot (D23). Others pointed 
to the need for communication (K6), the urge to remain 
visible on social media (K13), and the desire for peer 
approval (K17). For instance, some participants reported 
going online initially to connect with others, only to find 
themselves overwhelmed or distracted (K20). Others 
expressed that they felt invisible unless they were active 
on social platforms. As one participant explained:
Everyone sees me through my social media. If I don’t 
post something, if I don’t log in, it feels like no one 
will see me. (D26)
Within the scope of cognitive causes, participants identi
f
ied mental exhaustion, curiosity, and the perceived effi
ciency of quick information access as key contributors to 
their experience of brain rot (D109). Several described 
feeling too mentally fatigued to engage in sustained 
thinking, leading them to seek cognitive relief through 
digital media. Although this initially served to satisfy 
their curiosity or provide a sense of rest, many reported 
becoming passively absorbed in unrelated or trivial con
tent. As one participant put it:
I feel mentally exhausted, I just want to rest, and 
this pulls me into it. (K15)
T
his behavioral pattern was also evident during an obser
vation on October 08, 2024 in a philosophy of education 
class, where most students, when posed with reflec
tive questions, turned to phone applications rather than 
engaging in independent thought.

education
T
his section presents the effects of participants’ brain rot 
experiences on their education. As displayed in Table 5,

the findings are categorized under two main themes: 
impacts on educational readiness (affective, cognitive, 
and physical effects) and impacts on educational effi
ciency (cognitive, social, and time efficiency).
Under the educational readiness theme, the most pro
nounced effects of brain rot appeared in participants’ 
affective states. Participants consistently described diffi
culties with attention regulation and mental clarity. For 
instance, D17 noted, “I’m an unmindful user. I spend so 
much time on screens that my brain feels like it’s stopped. 
I experience constant attention deficits in class.” Simi
larly, K12 and K27 described feelings of cognitive stagna
tion and emotional numbness, suggesting a diminished 
capacity for sustained engagement. These challenges 
were also apparent during an observation on October 15, 
2024, in which students were frequently observed check
ing their phones during a lesson presentation, indicating 
fragmented attention and reduced focus.
Emotional impacts of brain rot were prominently 
reported and manifested in various interrelated forms. A 
recurrent theme across participants was attention defi
ciency and cognitive sluggishness, which often accom
panied broader mental health deterioration, including 
what several described as “mental fog.” K26 character
ized this condition as an addictive state: “I am an addict; I 
can’t do other activities anymore. Sometimes I don’t even 
eat.” Emotional exhaustion and burnout were frequently 
reported, as reflected in D59’s admission: “Sometimes 
I spend so much time on my phone that burnout never 
leaves me; I can’t care for my friends and family or ful
fil my responsibilities.” Feelings of depression, stress, and 
anxiety were also common. D35 stated, “My quality of life 
is quickly decreasing; my anxieties are increasing. I feel 
sadness and hopelessness.” Other participants reported 
a persistent sense of emptiness (K29), heightened regret 
and guilt over lost time and academic delays (K2), and 
a significant decline in self-confidence and self-worth 
(D46). For example, K8 described feeling disconnected

T

impairs individual academic functioning but also dis
rupts students’ social engagement and willingness to be 
actively involved in their educational environments.

Discussion
T
he study suggests that preservice teachers in the faculty 
of education may exhibit patterns of cognitive fatigue and 
reduced attention span, potentially influenced by a com
bination of emotional, habitual, environmental, social, 
and cognitive factors. Emotional drivers such as the 
desire to relieve boredom, the tendency to avoid respon
sibilities, the sense of incompleteness when disconnected 
from social media, the need to escape real-life challenges, 
fear of missing out (FOMO), and the pursuit of happiness 
through online platforms emerged as key contributors. 
Uğurlu [44] similarly found that boredom during free 
time significantly predicts social media addiction among 
university students. Twenge and Campbell [45] argued 
that digital addiction functions as an emotional coping 
mechanism among youth, echoing the present study’s 
f
indings. Przybylski et al. [46] introduced the concept of 
FOMO to describe the persistent concern about missing 
out on social interactions, which aligns with the partici
pants’ reported anxiety when offline. In addition, Meng 
and Leung [8] found that escapism and entertainment 
motives drive excessive use of platforms like TikTok, 
reinforcing the emotional underpinnings of brain rot 
observed in this study.
With regard to habitual factors, students reported 
using digital devices to fill free time, entertain them
selves, relax, and pursue convenience often in repetitive 
and unconscious ways. Notifications and likes in digital 
content cause the release of dopamine. Dopamine brings 
about the expectation of reward and, consequently, 
behavioral addiction [47]. These findings reflect Duhigg’s 
[48] habit loop theory, which explains how behaviors 
become automatic through cue-routine-reward cycles. 
T
he recurring use of digital media in idle moments sug
gests that brain rot, for many students, has become a 
deeply ingrained habit. This pattern aligns with [5], who 
found that entertainment motives are significant predic
tors of social media dependency. Uğurlu [44] further con
f
irmed that increased free time is associated with greater 
risk of social media addiction.
Environmental and social factors also contributed sub
stantially to brain rot. Participants cited the COVID-19 
pandemic, peer influence, the digitalization of educa
tion, and the accessibility and appeal of digital content as 
major drivers. UNESCO [49] similarly reported that the 
pandemic accelerated digital device dependency among 
youth, increasing vulnerability to addictive behaviors. 
Büyükgebiz Koca and Tunca [14] found that loneliness 
during the pandemic was linked to pathological inter
net use among young people, corroborating this study’s 
results. Students’ efforts to maintain online visibility 
and conform to digital norms reflect Festinger’s [50] 
Social Comparison Theory, which posits those individu
als evaluate themselves in comparison to others—often 
using social media as the primary reference point. Like
wise, Balcı et al. [5] emphasized that platforms like Tik
Tok are intentionally designed to maximize engagement 
and dependency. Cognitive contributors such as mental 
fatigue, heightened curiosity, and the desire for immedi
ate access to information also fueled brain rot. Dinç [51] 
identified curiosity as a central driver of internet addic
tion, while Kızılkaya [11] found that the search for psy
chological relief contributes to problematic digital use 
both findings consistent with this study.
Brain rot was also found to significantly affect stu
dents’ educational experiences, particularly in terms of 
educational readiness and productivity. This study pro
vides pre-service teachers with a valuable opportunity 
to enhance their awareness of brain rot and its potential 
impact on the educational process, thereby equipping 
them with an advantage in addressing related challenges 
T
his research allows pre-service teachers to gain an 
important advantage in overcoming problems caused by 
brain rot in the educational process by increasing their 
level of awareness of this concept [52]. Such awareness 
is crucial, as teachers who understand the cognitive and 
emotional effects of brain rot can act as positive role 
models for their future students, promoting healthier 
and more balanced digital behaviors. The findings, par
ticularly the frequent reports of attention deficits, men
tal fog, and emotional difficulties among participants, 
underscore the importance of integrating digital well
being education into teacher training programmes. Kan
dasamy et al. [17] identified similar effects in their work 
on internet addiction, highlighting mental, emotional, 
and physical consequences Within the scope of educa
tional readiness, brain rot causes cognitive decline, atten
tion deficit, mental fatigue, and fogginess. According to 
social cognitive theory, paying attention and encoding 
into memory are very important in the process of devel
oping behavior [53]. Unlike the results of this study, 
Lodge and Harrison [54] mention in their research that 
the effect of technology on attention is not unidirec
tional; for example, some video games may improve 
visual attention skills. The researchers revealed, in the 
context of attention learning theory, that attention is not 
a fixed ability and can be shaped through experience and 
the environment. Ophir et al. [55] found that constant 
exposure to digital media diminishes cognitive control 
and concentration. Recent findings by Lakilaki et al. [56] 
reinforce these conclusions, showing that digital overex
posure impairs attention and focus in academic settings. 
Consistent with the findings of this study, Sparrow et al. 
[57] demonstrated that the widespread use of the internet 
Gül BMC Psychology
           (2026) 14:98 
Page 12 of 15
fosters a form of cognitive offloading, leading to cogni
tive laziness and a diminished capacity for deep informa
tion processing. Similarly, Carr [52] argued that extensive 
internet use encourages superficial learning patterns and 
contributes to forgetfulness by weakening long-term 
memory consolidation. Recent research on brain rot fur
ther supports these perspectives, indicating that the phe
nomenon emerges as a consequence of excessive digital 
content consumption, which disrupts sustained attention 
and cognitive engagement. In this context, Adam & Cik 
Soh [31] revealed in their research that brain rot caused 
by excessive screen time can lead to problems such as 
memory issues, decreased attention span, and impaired 
decision-making. Nayak [32] noted that the concept of 
brain rot has become increasingly widespread in recent 
years and defined brain rot, as also demonstrated in this 
study, as a decline in cognitive functions and attention 
span. This decline caused by brain rot can lead to more 
serious problems, such as the transformation of cultural 
richness into consumable commodities and the superfi
cialization of cultural wealth [52].
T
he emotional toll of brain rot in this study marked by 
symptoms of depression, anxiety, emptiness, guilt, addic
tion, burnout, and reduced self-confidence echoes find
ings by Gökbulut and Bilen [58], who demonstrated that 
social media addiction correlates with elevated stress, 
anxiety, and depressive symptoms. Shen et al. [13] and 
Jaishy et al. [59] similarly reported that dysfunctional 
internet use leads to significant psychological distress. In 
their study on brain rot, Yousef et al. [33] concluded that 
brain rot has negative effects on mental health. These 
results indicate that brain rot may have long-term effects.
Physically, students described symptoms such as 
insomnia, neck and back pain, and eye strain condi
tions supported by Cain and Gradisar [60], who found 
that increased screen time disrupts sleep cycles, and by 
Achangwa et al. [61], who reported musculoskeletal dis
comfort among those with smartphone addiction. Yazgan 
[62] conducted a study on brain rot and found that the 
condition leads to sleeplessness and adversely affects 
learning and memory processes.
From an educational productivity perspective, brain 
rot was found to reduce students’ ability to comprehend 
course material, retain information, and engage in deep 
learning, while also contributing to procrastination and 
academic underperformance. Rathakrishnan et al. [16] 
similarly concluded that smartphone addiction negatively 
impacts academic achievement. Kirschner and Karpinski 
[22] found that frequent use of platforms like Facebook 
correlates with reduced study time and poorer academic 
outcomes. Sun and Chao [63] further demonstrated that 
multitasking with digital media leads to fragmented 
attention and diminished academic performance. These 
results align with Aznar-Díaz et al. [19], who identified 
a strong link between problematic internet use and aca
demic procrastination.
Socially, participants reported difficulties in maintain
ing peer relationships and initiating face-to-face interac
tions, contributing to isolation and reluctance to attend 
school. Han, Nagduar, and Yu [21] showed that digital 
addiction impairs interpersonal communication, while 
Achangwa et al. [61] highlighted its role in reducing real
life social engagement. Students in this study expressed 
reduced motivation to attend classes or engage in school 
activities, echoing Akbaba and Kaya’s [15] finding that 
problematic internet use lowers academic motivation. 
Finally, brain rot was found to disrupt students’ sense 
of time and time management. Gök [64] reported that 
students often use digital tools inefficiently, leading to 
significant time loss. Jojo and Sundaramoorthy [65] 
emphasized that weak time management skills often tied 
to a lack of personal responsibility are associated with 
excessive internet use, supporting this study’s findings. 
Owens [66] reported findings that diverge from those of 
the present study, concluding that young people perceive 
brain rot as digital engagement, and that the time spent 
in this process cannot be considered leisure time, as they 
believe this time provides them with relaxation. In addi
tion, Owens associated brain rot with earlier forms of 
purposeless entertainment, such as “killing time” or pas
sively watching television, arguing that the phenomenon 
should not be regarded as entirely new.
In response to these challenges, participants proposed 
several strategies to prevent brain rot, including educa
tional interventions, environmental restructuring, usage 
control, and the development of personal alternatives. 
Education was seen as a crucial tool in combating brain 
rot, with calls for digital literacy training, awareness cam
paigns on digital addiction, and guidance on responsible 
technology use. Aribowo and Bagaskara [67] found in a 
way that supports the results of this study, that brain rot 
is not only an individual problem but a structural issue 
produced by the digital ecology, and therefore, measures 
need to be taken at the societal level. These suggestions 
align with Achangwa et al. [61], who advocated for tar
geted education to mitigate smartphone addiction.
Participants also emphasized the importance of fam
ily dynamics and parental modeling [68] highlighted 
that internet addiction strains family relationships and 
that improved communication can serve as a protective 
factor. Recommendations for limiting screen time and 
implementing digital detox practices were also raised. 
Young [27] demonstrated the effectiveness of time 
restrictions in curbing internet addiction, while Varma 
[69] recommended structured detox periods as a preven
tive strategy.
In terms of educational practices, participants advised 
reducing digital content in classrooms, arguing that it

Page 13 of 15
exacerbates brain rot. Miniakhmetova et al. [70] sup
ported this concern, noting that increased digital learn
ing may heighten students’ susceptibility to digital 
dependency. Student-centered, collaborative classroom 
strategies were proposed as alternatives. Bağcı [1] simi
larly recommended engaging teaching methods to reduce 
digital reliance among university students.
Finally, students proposed developing personal alter
natives to screen use, such as engaging in arts, sports, 
and cultural activities, as well as participating in social 
events. These strategies were complemented by calls 
for increased self-regulation and personal responsibil
ity. Wibowo et al. [59] found that individuals with higher 
self-regulation are less susceptible to digital addiction, 
while Lakilaki et al. [56] observed that outdoor and 
physical activities can mitigate the effects of brain rot. 
Together, these findings underscore the importance of 
both systemic interventions and personal coping strate
gies in addressing the cognitive and educational risks 
associated with brain rot.
Recommendations
Schools could implement phone-use restrictions during 
specific hours or lessons to minimize digital distraction 
and support sustained attention. The use of applica
tions that monitor students’ daily screen time should be 
encouraged and made more widespread. Educators can 
foster deep reading habits by encouraging students to 
engage with printed materials more frequently and to 
practice slow, reflective reading to strengthen cognitive 
focus and comprehension.
T
his study lays the groundwork for quantitative 
research aimed at determining the wider prevalence of 
the “brain rot” phenomenon or for longitudinal studies 
that aim to track the long-term effects of digital habits. 
Furthermore, future cross-cultural comparative studies 
would provide valuable insights by revealing how digital 
behaviors and the manifestations of brain rot vary across 
different cultural and educational contexts.
Limitations
T
he study was conducted only at a single university in 
Türkiye; therefore, the findings may not be generaliz
able to broader populations or different cultural con
texts. Although data triangulation was employed, the 
screen-time data derived from participants’ self-reports 
may be subject to bias, as participants might not have 
reported their digital habits with complete accuracy. Fur
thermore, as the researcher actively conducted observa
tions throughout the study, this involvement may have 
introduced subjective influences during the interpreta
tion process, making it challenging to entirely eliminate 
researcher bias.
Although the study was conducted with 113 partici
pants, in-depth interviews were carried out with only 
30 individuals. While the findings indicate that the 
majority of participants exhibited symptoms of “brain 
rot,” detailed qualitative insights into the experiences of 
those who were not interviewed remained limited. This 
limitation was partially mitigated through the use of data 
triangulation.
Conclusions
T
his study on brain rot, a phenomenon emerging among 
students as a consequence of digital addiction, revealed 
that the condition arises from a complex interplay of 
emotional, habitual, environmental, social, and cogni
tive factors. Emotional factors included boredom and the 
desire for relaxation, avoidance of tasks or inactivity, feel
ings of deficiency when not engaging with social media, 
escapism from real-life problems, and happiness derived 
from online interactions. Habitual tendencies such as fill
ing free time, seeking entertainment or rest, and opting 
for convenience were also influential. Additionally, envi
ronmental, social, and cognitive reasons—such as the 
pandemic period, peer pressure, the digitalization of edu
cation, the appeal and accessibility of digital content, the 
desire for individuality, and anxieties regarding commu
nication and visibility further contributed to the devel
opment of brain rot. The findings indicate that brain rot 
manifests in educational contexts in two primary dimen
sions: educational readiness and educational efficiency. 
In terms of readiness, students suffering from brain rot 
reported attention deficits, mental fog, depression, anxi
ety, feelings of emptiness, regret or guilt, burnout, loss of 
self-confidence, impatience, social withdrawal, loneliness, 
difficulty focusing on lessons, cognitive decline, mental 
fatigue, forgetfulness, sleep disturbances, and physical 
symptoms such as eye strain and neck or back pain. In 
relation to educational efficiency, the condition led to 
challenges in comprehension and learning, a decline in 
academic achievement, procrastination, weakened social 
relationships, reluctance to attend school, and reduced 
engagement in educational activities. Furthermore, brain 
rot was found to undermine students’ time management, 
resulting in significant time loss and a distorted percep
tion of time.
